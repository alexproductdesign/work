Doubling conversion on a revenue-critical page

Trusted Shops offers consumers Buyer Protection for their online orders. The payment form – where shoppers upgrade to PLUS protection after a purchase – is one of the company's most direct revenue levers. Instead of betting everything on a single redesign, we ran a long-term experimentation program: 86 A/B tests over three years. I owned the design side of that program end to end.

Problem

A high-traffic page optimized on gut feeling

The payment form sat at the end of a delicate journey: a consumer buys something in a shop, receives a guarantee card, and lands on our page to upgrade their protection. Every design decision here directly moved revenue – but decisions were being made on opinions, not evidence. A full redesign was too risky: one wrong change on this page could cost more in a month than the entire team's budget.

How might we systematically improve a revenue-critical page – without ever risking the revenue itself?

Approach

A hypothesis engine, not a backlog of ideas

I turned the scattered stream of ideas – from stakeholders, C-level, legal, my own audits – into a repeatable design process:

01

Structured hypotheses

Every hypothesis in the same format: we observe X → by changing Y → metric Z moves by N%. No hypothesis, no experiment. This forced every idea (including the HiPPO ones) through the same gate.

02

Behavior patterns as design rationale

I grounded variants in consumer psychology – Paradox of Choice, Social Proof, Serial Position Effect, Goal Gradient, Picture Superiority – and tagged each experiment with the pattern it tested. Design decisions became arguable and teachable, not "looks better to me".

03

Prioritization

With limited traffic, we could only run so many tests. I prioritized by expected impact, effort and learning value – and killed ideas that couldn't articulate a hypothesis.

04

Device-level analysis

With two-thirds of traffic on mobile, I reviewed every result split by device. Several experiments won on one device and lost on the other – shipping the aggregate would have destroyed value.

05

Documented learnings

Wins and losses were written down with equal care. The learning library became input for the next round of hypotheses – and onboarding material for new team members.

Experiments

Five tests that tell the story

86 experiments produced dozens of learnings. These five defined how the page – and the program itself – work today.

Winner Paradox of choice

Less choice, more revenue

The form opened with two products side by side: cheap single-order protection and the PLUS subscription. The classic assumption says choice converts. Our observation said the opposite – users hesitated between two options and left without picking either.

The very first test of the program removed the cheaper product from the initial view entirely and offered it only as an exit intent, when a visitor was about to leave. Hiding a product felt counterintuitive – and increased total revenue. Consumers didn't need a comparison; they needed clarity.

→ "One product, one decision" shaped the page's product presentation for years.

Loser Rebrand

The failure that saved the redesign

When the new trstd brand arrived, the obvious move was to apply it to the form: new colors, new borders, same layout. I tested exactly that – and conversion dropped sharply. A conversion page can't be rebranded one cosmetic layer at a time: typography, color, spacing and content have to move together, or trust breaks.

Instead of shipping the brand refresh blind, I broke the redesign into small, individually tested steps – header, background color, progress bar, product card – each validated before the next went live. The rebrand shipped fully, without ever putting revenue at risk.

→ The cheap failure turned a big-bang redesign into a sequence of safe, measured steps.

Evidence Voice & tone

Data as a shield: the salutation debate

The company planned to switch every touchpoint from formal to informal address – a brand-wide decision that would have hit this page too. Our surveys showed the audience skews 55+, preferring formal salutation. So instead of arguing, I ran the test.

Informal address measurably hurt conversion. That single result gave the team hard evidence to protect a revenue-critical page from a company-wide branding decision – and the rollout was reconsidered.

→ An experiment can defend revenue better than any opinion in a meeting room.

Loser Localization

Scaling isn't copying: the NL lesson

Expanding to the Netherlands looked simple: take the bundle of proven German winners, translate, ship. The bundle lost on the NL form. Different market, different expectations – a translated winner isn't a winner.

The takeaway reshaped our rollout playbook: every market gets native-speaker localization and its own validation cycle before anything is declared "proven".

→ Winners don't travel. Validation does.

Winner · mobile only Serial position

The device split that changed how we read results

Moving legal content below the CTA won significantly on mobile – and did nothing on desktop. Small screens punish every extra block above the button; big screens don't care. Shipping the aggregate result would have averaged a real win into noise.

Results like this made device-level analysis a default step of every readout, not an afterthought – with two-thirds of our traffic on mobile, the split often was the story.

→ Every result gets read per device before it gets shipped.

Evolution

From ad-hoc tests to an operating system

Over three years I redesigned the program as much as the page:

Guardrails

Flickering in above-the-fold tests, broken shop logos tanking conversion for individual shops, legal reviews happening too late – each incident became a checklist item applied to future experiment designs.

Stakeholders

Legal and localization moved from post-hoc reviewers to pre-launch participants.

Prototyping

For later experiments I used AI-assisted prototyping (Figma Make) to test concepts like comparison tables without dev support – then rebuilt winners properly in the design system for rollout.

Tooling

Migration from AB Tasty to Kameleoon without losing the experiment history or comparability.

Outcome

Compounding beats redesigning

No single experiment doubled the conversion rate – the system did. The form's conversion grew from ~6% to ~13%, while the page was gradually rebranded, legally hardened, and extended to a second market, all without a single risky big-bang launch.

Not everything worked: roughly a third of my experiments lost or came out flat, and several "obvious" best practices – bigger fonts, extra trust badges, comparison tables above the fold – failed against our audience. That was the point: the losers were cheap, contained, and informative.

On a revenue-critical page, taste is an opinion. A significance level is an argument.
